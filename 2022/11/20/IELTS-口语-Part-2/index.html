Part 2 and part 3 - Ideas, Model Answers and Analysis

EVENT

Describe a happy event you organised
You should say:

  • What it was
  • When it happened
  • How you organised it

and explain how you felt about organising the event

Ideas and Vocabulary

You could talk about…

A party - such as work, new baby, birthday, anniversary,
retirement, wedding, stag do or hen night
Picnic or barbecue
Sports event (e.g. a football match with friends)

Remember to focus more on the event, and your role

  • Events
    • Indoor event
    • Outdoor event
    • To set up (an event) = to organise
    • To be responsible for… / It was up to me to…
      • To take into consideration dietary requirements
  • Preparation
    • To take care of / to look after
    • To have a lot on your plate
      • I had a lot on my plate
    • To over-prepare
    • To play it by ear = not to plan (improvise)
      • I decided to play it by ear

Model Answers

So, the event I’d like to describe was a birthday party I helped organise for my daughter and her friends. She had just turned 6 (to become 6 years old), if I remember correctly, and we had invited a bunch of friends from school.
So my wife and I split up the responsibilities (divide the responsibilities). It was up to me to organise the games and the fun actives and my wife took care of the invitations and she looked after liaising (contact and cooperate) with the other parents.
I remember, I had to really rack my brains (think hard to get an answer) to think of games and activities that they would enjoy, but where things wouldn’t get out of hand (get out of control) either. I mean you probably know how boisterous (noisy and energetic) and unpredictable (changeable, surprising) young children can be.
So, in the end, I set up some fun games, like ‘pass the parcel’ where they have to pass the gift round and unwrap it as they go. On top of that (besides), we had a pizza-making session, a time to give presents, and of course, the must-have birthday cake moment where my daughter had to blow out (put out, extinguish a fire) the candles on the cake.
I do remember also I had to take into consideration the safety of all the children, with the noise, the oven for cooking the pizza and things like that.
All said and done, (in the end, at the end of the day, after considering everything) it went very well. In fact, it went without a hitch (go smoothly, without problems). I can proudly say nobody was injured at the party and there were no tears. The kids seemed to have a whale of a time (have a great time), and my daughter was thrilled to bits (be very excited and happy) with the whole party.

Part 3

  1. On what occasions do people need to be organised?
  2. How can parents help children be organised?
    1. Give an opinion -> Give an example -> Give a reason

    2. I don’t have any hands-on experience (real-life experience) with this but… (OR - Well, I can tell you from first hand experience (direct experience) and…) …in my opinion parents can set a good example (give a good model to follow). For instance, as well as telling their children to make their bed, parents should first make their own bed, so children can see what they should be doing and then follow suit (do the same). On top of that, I think parents should set clear routines (clear limits) for their children to follow. Kids like to have clear boundaries and this can help them organise their time.

  3. Does everything we do need to be well prepared?
    1. Give an opinion -> Give an example -> Give a reason

    2. It depends on the situation and your personality. I think some people thrive on (enjoy and succeed doing something others may not like) improvising and doing things on the fly (do instantly without preparation). I have heard of some great CEOs who rarely prepare meetings, they prefer to just go with the flow (to let things happen naturally), maybe the reason is they feel too much preparation stifles their creativity. That said, other people may feel more confident if they prepare, say a meeting or a speech, well. For them, I guess playing it by ear (to improvise , do without preparation) is just too risky.

  4. Do people need other peoples’ help when organising things?

CLOTHES

Describe a person who you think wears unusual clothes.
You should say:

  • who this person is
  • how you know about this person
  • what this person’s clothes are like

and explain why you think this person’s clothes are unusual.

Ideas and Vocabulary

You could talk about…

Someone you know or someone famous
Teacher
Friend
Colleague at work
Celebrity

  • Wearing Clothes
    • to wear, to put on a shirt, to sport my best tie for the job interview
    • clothing (type/situation)
      • winter clothing
    • an outfit
    • attire (formal)
    • gear (informal)
      • climbing gear, swimming, sports, running
  • Unusual Clothes
    • To wear _____ clothes
      • eccentric
      • bizarre
      • off-beat
      • unconventional
      • weird
      • quirky

        He wears casual clothes
        I wear comfortable clothing at home
        He likes to sport colourful ties
        I have so many outfits, I’m spoilt for choice!
        I sometimes wear my running gear at home

Model Answers

I’m going to tell you about (signpost) my history teacher at university.
You know, he was, and still is, a bit of an eccentric (strange, unconventional), especially when it comes to clothes. I would say his clothes are unconventional, to say the least. Many students think he is just weird.
To be honest, he doesn’t seem to care what others think of his taste in clothes. I actually think he sees himself as being quite fashionable. So let me try to describe his rather offbeat style to you. For starters (First of all), he always wears odd colour socks.
This must be on purpose (deliberately), because I can’t see how he could wear mismatching socks everyday, if it were by accident (by mistake).
Next, his trousers always seem to be too short, that’s how you notice his socks, and more often than not (usually, most times), he wears braces rather than a belt. Maybe because he is rather stout and chubby (plump, rather fat). Unfortunately, it makes him look a bit like a clown.
Finally, he tends to sport flamboyant (exuberant) shirts with complex patterns and bright colours. For me, his clothes don’t really match, I mean the colours don’t go well together (to match), if I’m honest (honestly speaking). But he seems very comfortable and at ease (comfortable and relaxed) in what I would call very quirky attire (unusual clothes).

Part 3

  1. Do clothes affect a person’s mood?
  2. What are the key factors to consider when buying clothes?

    1. Well, first of all, many people undoubtedly consider the price.
      I think we are creatures of habit, right? and we are always looking for bargains, and trying to get a discount.
      Not because we lack money, but I think it’s just a habit we have. When
      we buy things at a knockdown price, we always feel we have got a good deal (a good bargain).
      At the same time, comfort is a prime concern. It’s all well and good (this is good, but) buying cheap or trendy (fashionable) clothes, but if you are not comfy in them, you are not going to wear them much.

  3. Do you think men and women have different taste in clothes?
  4. How are the clothes young people wear today, different from the past?

    1. To tell the truth, I don’t actually think there is that much difference.
      Young people, whatever the generation, tend to be more fashion conscious. They just follow different fashions, depending on the era.
      I mean for example, when my father was young, denim jackets and flared jeans were all the rage (very fashionable), if you were a rocker.
      Nowadays, many teenagers in my country wear distressed jeans (ripped jeans), you know with holes and frays.

TRUTH

Describe a time when you told your friend an important truth.
You should say:

  • who your friend is
  • what the truth was
  • how your friend reacted

and explain how you felt about it.

Ideas and Vocabulary

You could talk about…

  • Expressing your real feelings - (don’t bottle up your feelings)
    • Tell your friend - you are angry, sad, irritated, depressed, happy…
  • You don’t like something they do / did / said
    • Bad habit (smoking)
    • They gossiped, told a secret, lied…
  • Praising them
  • Confessing you did something wrong
  • Telling The Truth
    • To speak up / out
    • Be brave enough to speak out
    • To be up front, to be frank / candid, to share your feelings, to be open, to be crystal clear, to speak plainly
  • Idioms
    • I didn’t beat about the bush = I went straight to the point
    • I didn’t go all around the houses = I went straight to the point

Model Answers

Let me tell you about a time I had to tell reveal my true feelings to my friend about his habit of smoking.
So, Martin, who’s been a friend since we were kids, is now married with a kid and smokes a lot.
I mean he’s practically a chain smoker.
It got worse when his baby was born, I guess it was partly stress-related.
It troubled me (worried me, concerned me) because I knew smoking was bad for his health and bad for those around him, due to passive smoking, right?
So I decided I should say something.
To be honest, it was really hard to build up the courage to speak out and I kept putting it off (delay).
I didn’t want him to be angry and of course, there was the risk of this damaging our friendship (the risk of you having an accident, the risk of me losing my bike, the risk of the conversation damaging our relationship).
Finally, I found a good moment to talk to him and I was just really up front about my feelings (speak honestly about), about the harm he was doing to himself and above all to his baby.
All said and done (in the end), he actually took it very well (accept bad news well).
He didn’t fly off the handle (get very angry) or anything, he agreed with me and said he would try and stop.
So, I was delighted with him and with myself, if the truth be told (honestly speaking), because I had managed to have a difficult conversation and it had all turned out well (finish well).

Part 3

  1. Do you think people should always tell the truth?

    1. That’s a tricky question. By and large yes, but you don’t want to always tell the gospel truth (tell the whole truth), and there are times when a white lie (a small lie) maybe a better option. For example, if somebody invites me to their house for dinner and I don’t like the food they have cooked, I will probably tell a fib (tell a small lie), perhaps saying it has an unusual taste, and it’s really interesting to try new dishes. I don’t think that’s deceitful (to mislead, to lie), it’s just being polite.

  2. How can we teach children about the importance of telling the truth?
  3. Why do some people cheat when playing games?

    1. Maybe they are just extremely competitive and don’t like losing. When it comes to games, I guess they prioritise winning over honesty. Look at football players in the big championship matches, right? They fake falling and things like that because so much is at stake (is at risk). Generally speaking, I suspect that people cheat because they think they can get away with it (not be found out). If nobody catches them, then nobody is any wiser (nobody knows about it) and they think ‘no harm done’!

  4. Is cheating sometimes acceptable?

RISK

Describe a time you took a risk that may have gone wrong, but turned out well.
You should say,

  • what it was
  • when you took it
  • what happened

And explain why it turned out well

Ideas and Vocabulary

You could talk about…

A choice of …
…home (buying/renting)
…job
…partner
…investment
Choice of holiday destination - hotel - restaurant
Choice when buying clothes / book

Moving/changing to another city, country, job,
Ignore someone’s advice (especially advice from parents/friends)
for… work/study/marriage/home-buying

  • Risk
    • A risk (n.)
    • To take a risk
    • To risk (v.) something
    • To take a calculated risk
    • A high-risk / low-risk investment
    • Take a big / small risk
  • Risk collocations
    • A risk-taker
    • To be risk averse
    • A risk pays off (for me)
    • Trust your gut instincts
    • To play it safe
    • To stick your neck out = take a big risk
    • It’s a safe bet = it’s very low-risk
    • To mitigate the risk = to reduce the risk

Model Answers

I’d like to tell you about the time I took a new job in a new country.
I am not a big risk taker, but I thought this was a calculated risk (balanced risk) that would allow me to build my career. I must have been (I think I was) about 30 at the time, and I applied for a new job to work as a school director in China.
After the online interview I was accepted for the job.
Looking back it was extremely risky. I really stuck my neck out (took a big risk).
I didn’t really know Chinese, I had never heard of the city I was going to, and I didn’t know a soul (knew nobody) in the country.
So, I would be starting from scratch (to begin again with nothing), with no connections, no network, nothing.
I did mitigate the risk by learning some Chinese before I went, and also by just taking a one-year contract.
If it all went belly up (go wrong (often financially)), I could just go back home and look for a job in England.
On top of that, the company I was working for was a global company, with a pretty good reputation. So, I thought it would be a safe bet (be sure or very low-risk).
In the end, it turned out quite well (finish well / have a good result).
Yes, it was challenging, but I learnt loads (a lot), not only about China, but about myself too.
I actually ended up staying in China for a number of years, and it really helped me grow my career. So, it was a risk, but one that definitely paid off for me (It was worth doing).

Part 3

  1. Why do you think young people take risks more than elderly people?

    1. I am not sure all young people are risk takers, but by and large I think the older we get, the more risk averse we become. Mainly because, we have more responsibilities as we get older, you know we have to raise our families, pay off mortgages (after buying a house) and the like. Young people on the other hand, are generally more footloose and fancyfree (no responsibilities or cares), so they have less to lose. If things go wrong, they can bounce back (recover) quite easily. They have more energy and often have family who can support them.

  2. Do you agree boys are more likely to take risks than girls?
  3. Should we do our own research when faced with dangerous situations?
  4. What advice should parents give their children about taking risks?

    1. Well, that will depend largely on the parent’s attitude to risk. By and large, I think they should encourage their children to take calculated risks, because it is by taking risks that we learn and grow. For example, if you apply for a promotion, you will benefit and learn new things, whether you get the job or not. That said, I reckon parents should teach their children how to evaluate risks, so they can avoid doing irresponsible things. For instance, hitch-hiking (ask for a ride in the car of a person you don’t know) alone through Mexico might be a great adventure, but the risk of being attacked, robbed or worse is high, and probably not worth it (the benefit is less than the cost).
